## What Is SHA-256?

SHA-256 (Secure Hash Algorithm 256-bit) is the cryptographic hash function at the core of Bitcoin's proof-of-work system. It was originally designed by the NSA and published by NIST in 2001 as part of the SHA-2 family. Bitcoin applies it twice in sequence — a process known as double SHA-256 — to produce the 256-bit block header hash that miners must beat a target difficulty. When Satoshi Nakamoto chose SHA-256 for Bitcoin's genesis block in 2009, the goal was security and simplicity: the algorithm is well-studied, fast to compute, and deterministic, making fraud easy to detect and valid work easy to verify.

Mining SHA-256 is a brute-force numbers game. Miners increment a nonce in the block header millions of times per second until the resulting hash starts with enough leading zeros to satisfy the current network difficulty. The only way to win is raw hash throughput — there are no shortcuts.

## ASIC Resistance

SHA-256 has essentially zero ASIC resistance. Purpose-built ASICs outperform CPUs by more than a billion to one in efficiency. GPU mining SHA-256 has been economically unviable since 2013. Today the entire SHA-256 network is dominated by large-scale ASIC farms — solo mining with consumer hardware is statistically impossible. This consolidation is a known tradeoff: Bitcoin prioritizes security and decentralization at the protocol level rather than at the hardware level.
